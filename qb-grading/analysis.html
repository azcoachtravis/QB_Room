QB Grading System Analysis
Are the existing columns (Pass Decision · Pass Pressure · Pass Pocket · Pass Release · Pass Throw · Run Decision · Run Fake · QB Impact · QB Note) adequate for the SOCKS system? Will they produce a PFF-style dashboard that accurately represents quarterback performance?
v1.2 Research: PFF Methodology High School QB Grading SOCKS Integration Analysis
Bottom Line Verdict
The existing 9-column system is mostly adequate but has three meaningful gaps relative to what the SOCKS system actually develops and what a PFF-style dashboard needs to be accurate and actionable. The columns cover the execution side (throw quality, pocket, release) well — these map cleanly to PFF methodology. The gaps are on the pre-snap and situational side, which is exactly what SOCKS is built to develop. Specifically: there is no direct grade for pre-snap read quality, no separation between QB-caused and scheme-caused pressure, and no YAC/placement grade that captures where the ball was thrown — only whether it was thrown. These three additions would make the dashboard genuinely PFF-comparable and directly reflect what SOCKS is teaching at every stage.
How PFF Grades Quarterbacks — What Actually Matters

PFF gives each player a grade from –2 to +2 on every play, with 0 representing the average or "expected" level of performance. The critical philosophical principle — and the one most relevant to your system — is this: the result of the play does not change the quarterback's grade.

A pinpoint downfield pass that is dropped still earns the same positive grade. A completion on a reckless throw still earns a negative grade. This is the most important thing to internalize: PFF is grading the QB's contribution to production, not the outcome. A dropped pass on a perfect throw is still a perfect throw.

The PFF grading system isolates the quarterback's play — his decision, timing, and accuracy on a throw — all baked into the play-by-play grade. Misreading coverage and firing a pass to a linebacker results in the same grade whether the linebacker catches it for an interception or drops it.

PFF separates QB grading into distinct buckets: decision-making, accuracy/ball placement, pocket presence, and pressure performance. Grades are then converted to a 0–100 scale at the game and season level, with the average for every position meant to be a 60. For your purposes — high school development — the play-by-play grades are more valuable than the aggregate scale.

The two signature PFF metrics for QBs are Big-Time Throws (grades of +1.0 or better) and Turnover-Worthy Plays (grades of –1.0 or worse). Passes graded at –1.0 or worse receive the "turnover-worthy" label because interceptions occur on about 50% of such throws — and that percentage rises as the grade lowers. Your system already captures this with the Pass Decision column's Elite/Reckless scale, which is the correct instinct.

The Three Gaps — What's Missing for SOCKS + PFF Accuracy
Gap 1 — No Pre-Snap Read Grade (the most important missing piece)
What's missing: None of the 9 columns directly grades the quality of the QB's pre-snap process — his coverage ID, shell read, key identification, and situational awareness. This is the entire SOCKS framework and it has no home in the current grading system.

Why it matters for PFF accuracy: A QB who makes a correct throw on a wrong read is getting credit he didn't earn. A QB who makes a correct throw on a perfect pre-snap read should get more credit. The current system can't distinguish these two outcomes — they produce identical grades.

Why it matters for SOCKS: Stage 2 (Safeties), Stage 3 (Leverage), and Stage 4 (Full SOCKS + Situational) are entirely about what happens before the snap. Without a pre-snap grade, your dashboard cannot tell you whether a QB's good decisions are happening because of SOCKS or in spite of it.

Proposed addition: PRE-SNAP READ — Elite (2), Neutral (1), Poor (0). Elite = correct shell ID, coverage named, key identified, situation applied. Neutral = adequate. Poor = wrong coverage ID or no pre-snap verbalization. This single column would tie the dashboard directly to SOCKS stage development.
Gap 2 — No Ball Placement / YAC Grade
What's missing: Pass Throw grades whether the ball was thrown well. It does not grade where the ball was thrown — specifically whether placement created or eliminated yards after the catch.

Why it matters for PFF accuracy: PFF accounts for how much a completion asks of the receiver. Ball placement that allows a receiver to catch without breaking stride earns more credit than a throw that forces a significant adjustment. A complete pass to the inside shoulder that forces the receiver to turn back toward the defense and lose 3 yards of YAC is not the same as a complete pass to the outside shoulder in stride.

Why it matters for SOCKS: Stage 3 is specifically about reading leverage to identify WHERE the space is — and then throwing to that space. Without a YAC/placement grade, you have no way to measure whether your QB is actually applying the leverage read to his delivery point. Pass Throw says he completed it. YAC/Placement says he completed it to the right spot.

Proposed addition: BALL PLACEMENT — Outside shoulder in stride (+), Catchable/neutral (0), Forced adjustment (–). Could be folded into Pass Throw as a modifier, or added as a stand-alone column for completions only (same as PFF's approach).
Gap 3 — Pass Pressure Ambiguity (context vs. grade)
What's missing: Clarity on whether Pass Pressure is recording a context (defense sent extra rushers) or grading the QB's response to pressure. These are two different things and conflating them corrupts the dashboard.

The PFF approach: Pressure is a context flag. The QB's grade under pressure comes from his Pass Decision and Pass Throw scores on pressured plays — split separately in the dashboard. Whether he was pressured is the input; how he performed under it is the output.

Current risk: If "Pressure" in Pass Pressure means "the QB was under pressure and it affected the play," that's grading him for something partly outside his control. If it means "the defense blitzed," that's correct context. The rubric needs to be explicit: Pass Pressure = what the defense did, not what the QB did. The QB's response to pressure is already captured in Pass Decision (did he get the ball out?), Pass Pocket (did he handle the pocket?), and Pass Release (did he get it out on time?).

Proposed clarification: Rename to PRESSURE CONTEXT and make it a context-only flag: Neutral / Blitzed / Throwaway / Spike / Scramble / Sack. Then build dashboard splits — Pass Decision on Blitz plays vs. clean plays — rather than trying to grade pressure performance as a single column.
SOCKS Stage → Column Integration Map

Every SOCKS stage should produce measurable improvement in specific grade columns. Here is how the current columns map — and where the gaps appear in red.

Stage 1 — Count + Key
SOCKS: C + K → Give/Keep decision
The box count and key identification determines the RPO trigger. A QB mastering Stage 1 should show improvement in Run Decision (correct give/keep) and Pass Decision (correct trigger throw). These two columns capture Stage 1 development well.
Stage 2 — Safeties + Shell
SOCKS: S → Shell ID → Blitz recognition
Shell identification (MOFO/MOFC) and blitz recognition should show in Pass Decision quality under pressure. But without a Pre-Snap Read grade, you can't confirm the improvement came from the shell read vs. luck or athleticism. This is Gap 1.
⚠ Gap: No pre-snap grade captures shell ID improvement
Stage 3 — Corners + Space
SOCKS: O + S → Leverage → YAC window
CB leverage reads should show in Pass Throw (better ball placement) and would ideally show in a Ball Placement/YAC grade. Without it, a QB who reads leverage correctly but delivers to the wrong shoulder gets the same Pass Throw grade as one who nails both. This is Gap 2.
⚠ Gap: No YAC/Placement grade captures leverage-to-throw improvement
Stage 4 — Full SOCKS
SOCKS: Full scan → Situation → Anticipation
Full SOCKS mastery should show across all columns — particularly Pass Decision moving toward Elite more often (anticipatory throws), and improved performance on 3rd down and in the red zone (situational filter). If Pre-Snap Read were added, Stage 4 QBs should show Pre-Snap = Elite on 80%+ of plays.
⚠ Gap: Situational context (down, hash, score) has no dedicated column
Can the Current System Produce a PFF-Style Dashboard?
What the Current System Does Well
Comparable to PFF methodology
Process over result — grading the throw, not the completion
Pass Decision scale (Elite/Reckless) mirrors PFF's BTT/TWP framework
Pocket presence column separates QB-caused from coverage-caused
Release timing is a legitimate standalone — release is a product of footwork and vision including pre-snap and post-snap thought processes
Pressure as context (if used correctly) enables clean vs. pressured splits
Run Fake is a high-school-specific addition PFF doesn't have — genuinely valuable
QB Note enables gray area documentation just like PFF's analyst notes
Where the Dashboard Falls Short of PFF
What would make it genuinely comparable
No pre-snap read grade — PFF's system is built on process, not just execution
No ball placement / YAC differentiation — PFF explicitly separates throw accuracy from placement quality
No situational filter — PFF's research shows grades on 3rd down, red zone, and 2-minute drill provide better predictive value
Pass Pressure may be functioning as a grade rather than a context flag — this corrupts pressure splits
QB Impact, if overused, dilutes the column's signal — should be a rare flag
No Big-Time Throw rate metric — PFF's BTT% is one of the most predictive QB statistics

Bottom line: With the current 9 columns, you can produce a useful and meaningful dashboard. With three additions — Pre-Snap Read, Ball Placement, and a cleaner Pressure Context flag — you would have a dashboard that is genuinely comparable to PFF's methodology at the high school level, and more importantly, one that directly measures what SOCKS is built to develop at every stage.

Proposed Additions — Minimal, High-Value
Addition 1 — PRE-SNAP READ (required for SOCKS integration)
Column: PRE-SNAP READ
Scale: Elite (2) / Neutral (1) / Poor (0)
When to grade: Every play
Elite (2): QB correctly identified the shell (MOFO/MOFC), named the coverage, identified the key, and applied situational context before the snap. Could verbalize if asked.
Neutral (1): Adequate read. QB made the correct decision but the pre-snap process was not explicitly demonstrated or verifiable.
Poor (0): Wrong coverage ID, wrong key, missed blitz indicator, or no visible pre-snap process.
SOCKS link: Stage 1 = look for correct key ID (C+K). Stage 2 = shell ID quality (S). Stage 3 = leverage read applied (O). Stage 4 = situational context verbalized. Pre-Snap Read = Elite should increase as QB advances stages.
Addition 2 — BALL PLACEMENT (required for Stage 3 measurement)
Column: BALL PLACEMENT (or fold into Pass Throw)
Scale: Elite (+) / Neutral (0) / Poor (–)
When to grade: Pass completions only — same as PFF's YAC component
Elite (+): Outside shoulder, receiver catches in stride going away from the defender. Created YAC by design.
Neutral (0): Catchable, receiver must stop or adjust slightly. No YAC benefit but no damage.
Poor (–): Inside shoulder, behind receiver, or placement forced the receiver back toward the defense. Eliminated YAC.
SOCKS link: Stage 3 — "leverage tells you WHERE to throw." A QB who reads the leverage correctly but throws to the wrong shoulder is a Stage 3 execution failure. This column catches it. Without it, that failure is invisible in the data.
Clarification — PASS PRESSURE → PRESSURE CONTEXT (column rename + rubric fix)
Rename to: PRESSURE CONTEXT
Scale: Neutral / Blitzed / Throwaway / Spike / Sack / Scramble / RPO Glance / Screen / Checkdown
This is NOT a grade: It is a context tag that enables the dashboard to split Pass Decision and Pass Throw into clean-pocket vs. under-pressure groups. The QB's performance under pressure is already captured in the other columns — this just flags which plays were pressured.
Dashboard impact: Add two summary stats — "Pass Decision avg (clean pocket)" and "Pass Decision avg (when blitzed)." The gap between those two numbers is your pressure performance metric, and it's far more informative than a single Pressure column grade.
SOCKS link: Pressure Override applies at all stages. The clean vs. blitz split tells you whether the QB's hot route recognition is working. If his Pass Decision drops significantly on blitz plays, his Stage 2 pre-snap ID is the problem.
Final Recommendation — What to Do
Keep as-is (strong, don't change)
Pass Decision — your best column, directly mirrors PFF's most important QB metric.
Pass Throw — solid, just needs the rubric to explicitly address ball placement direction.
Pass Pocket — good, clarify that Poor Pocket = QB-caused only.
Pass Release — unique value, directly tied to SOCKS throw clock.
Run Decision + Run Fake — both valid, both SOCKS-aligned.
QB Note — keep free text for gray areas.
Fix rubric / rename (existing column, new clarity)
Pass Pressure → Pressure Context: Make explicit it's a context flag, not a grade. The QB's response to pressure lives in Decision, Pocket, and Release — not here.
QB Impact: Reserve for genuinely game-changing plays. If it's applied to more than 5–8% of plays, it's being overused and losing signal. Neutral Impact = the correct default.
Add (fills the SOCKS gap, required for meaningful dashboard)
PRE-SNAP READ (0–2): Elite / Neutral / Poor. Grade every play. This single column ties the dashboard directly to SOCKS stage development. A QB who advances from Stage 1 to Stage 4 should show Pre-Snap = Elite moving from 20% to 80%+ of plays over his career.

BALL PLACEMENT (–/0/+): Outside shoulder in stride / Catchable / Forced adjustment. Grade completions only. This is the column that measures whether the QB is applying Stage 3 leverage reads to his delivery point — the most important and most invisible skill in your system.
