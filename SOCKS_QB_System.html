Stage 1
Count + Key — Building the Foundation
Typical: Freshman
S · O · C · K · S
SOCKS Focus: Count + Key
What Stage 1 Builds: The foundation. Before a QB can read a safety or identify leverage, he must count the box and find the key defender. Every rep at Stage 1 produces one output: the QB knows who to watch before the snap.
Coaching Cue
"Trust the count."
Prerequisites
Formation Knowledge
  • Knows what the OL is doing
  • Can identify the 5 blockers
  • Understands what the "box" is
Basic Play Knowledge
  • Understands the play call
  • Knows run vs. pass assignments
  • Can execute a handoff cleanly
Eyes Up at the LOS
  • Head is up pre-snap
  • Looking at the defense
  • Holds cadence while scanning
Teaching Goals — Chronological Order
  1. What is the Box? Foundation
    Define the box as the area between the tackles and 5 yards deep. QB visually frames the box every snap. Must become automatic before anything else is taught.
  2. Count Defenders in the Box Core Skill
    Count from inside out. The 5 OL are the baseline. Show a defensive alignment — QB calls the count in 2 seconds. He has 4.5 seconds from huddle break to snap (Pop-Tart rule).
  3. The 3 Box Count Scenarios Core Skill
    5/4 → Run advantage. Numbers say give. 5/5 → Even box. Read the DE (1st level). 5/6 → Defense has the extra hat. Read the Apex/Backer (2nd level). Teach 5/6 first — most common RPO trigger at the HS level.
  4. Identify the Key Defender Core Skill
    After the count, QB immediately locates the key. Teach him to physically point to the key in practice. "Who am I watching?" must have an instant answer every rep.
  5. The Trigger Concept Decision Making
    If the key triggers (steps toward the run), the window is open — throw. If he stays wide — give. Binary. Teach as if/then: key triggers = throw. Key is wide = give. No hesitation after the trigger fires.
  6. The Basic RPO Decision Tree Execution
    Count → Find key → Watch trigger → Throw or give. Run this out loud every rep. QB verbalizes each step. Hesitation after the trigger is the most common mistake — it is a reaction, not a decision.
  7. Down & Distance Awareness Situational Intro
    Before every snap, the QB states the down and distance. Not strategy yet — habit. Ask him before every rep. This plants the seed for Situational Awareness that develops fully at Stages 3 and 4.
  8. Quick Game Timing — 1.8 Second Clock Timing
    On quick game (3-step), the ball leaves at the last step of the drop — approximately 1.8 seconds. Use a stopwatch early, then remove it. Ball out on time — not held, not rushed.
Stage 1 Milestones

80%+ accuracy over 10 reps, across three evaluation sessions.

  • Box count correctly identified in 2 seconds or less — every rep
  • Correct key defender named immediately after count
  • Trigger concept explained without coaching prompts
  • Correct RPO decision 8 of 10 live reps
  • Down and distance stated before every snap
  • Ball out within 1.8 seconds quick game — 7 of 10 reps
  • Full decision flow verbalized: Count → Key → Trigger → Execute
Common Mistakes at Stage 1
  • Counting too slowly — still scanning at the snap
  • Finding the count but forgetting to locate the key
  • Hesitating after the trigger fires — window closes
  • Looking at the receiver before watching the key
  • Holding the ball past 1.8s on quick game
  • Forgetting down and distance mid-drive
Advancement Criteria

All 7 milestones at 80%+ across three sessions (film room, cadence reads, live reps). See Page 7 for the full scoring rubric.

"Trust the count." — Numbers tell you who to read. Everything starts here.

Stage 2
Safeties + Shell — Adding the Top of the Defense
Typical: Sophomore
S · O · C · K · S
SOCKS Focus: Safeties + Count + Key
What Stage 2 Builds: The QB already knows how to count and find the key. Now he adds the safeties. MOFO vs. MOFC tells him the defensive shell before he looks at the box. Motion enters here as the lie detector for man vs. zone coverage.
Coaching Cue
"Safeties don't lie.
Corners do."
Prerequisites — Stage 1 Complete
Count is Automatic

Box count in under 2 seconds — a reflex, not a process. Must be automatic before adding safeties.

Key is Instant

Locates key defender immediately after counting — no pause, no searching.

Trigger Decision Clean

80%+ RPO decisions in live reps. No hesitation. All Stage 1 milestones achieved.

Teaching Goals — Chronological Order
  1. MOFO vs. MOFC — Shell Identification New Skill
    MOFO = 2 safeties deep (Cover 2/4). MOFC = 1 high safety (Cover 1/3). First look post-huddle is always to the safeties. Teach as a 0.5-second glance. Use film — QB calls MOFO or MOFC on 20 clips. Speed matters.
  2. What the Shell Tells You About Run Support Concept
    MOFC = one safety closer to the run box = faster inside support. MOFO = run fitters come from the boundary safety and nickel. MOFC + 5/6 = high probability the key triggers.
  3. Layering Safeties Over the Count Integration
    The scan becomes: safeties first → shell ID → then count. The QB says "MOFC — 5/6 — Apex is my key" as a single fluid statement. That phrase IS the plan.
  4. Apex/Backer Behavior Based on Shell Decision Making
    Against MOFC, the Apex/Backer at 5/6 has a run fit — he is coming. The trigger is faster and more certain. The QB's read doesn't change, but his confidence in the trigger does.
  5. Blitz Recognition — The Hot Principle Protection
    MOFC confirmed, now add a blitz check. Safeties rolling down, LBs creeping, CB press = potential blitz. If blitz is identified, execute the hot route. Pressure overrides key hierarchy — this becomes critical at Stages 3 and 4.
  6. Motion Introduction — Man vs. Zone Motion
    "Motion tells the truth — man follows, zone rotates." A defender trailing motion = confirmed man. Secondary rotating = confirmed zone. At Stage 2, the QB reads motion — he does not yet deploy it.
  7. Extending the Throw Clock — Add 2.2 Seconds Timing
    Stage 1 was quick game at 1.8s. Stage 2 adds the drop-back read at 2.2 seconds — hitch or shuffle step after the initial drop. Drill both clocks: 1.8s quick game and 2.2s confirmation plays.
  8. Score and Game Clock Awareness Situational
    Add score and clock to the down/distance habit from Stage 1. Before every snap: down, what we need, and score/time situation. Ask before every rep: "What does this play need to accomplish right now?"
Stage 2 Milestones
  • MOFO vs. MOFC identified in under 1 second — 9 of 10 film reps
  • Full pre-snap call verbalized: MOFC · Cover 1 · 5/6 · Apex is my key
  • Apex/Backer behavior correctly predicted by shell — 80%+ live
  • Blitz identified pre-snap and hot route executed — 7 of 10
  • Motion read (man vs. zone) correct — 8 of 10 reps
  • Drop-back read timing at 2.2s — 7 of 10 reps
  • Score and clock stated before every snap
  • Stage 1 milestones maintained at 80%+ — no regression
Common Mistakes at Stage 2
  • Looking at corners first instead of safeties
  • Forgetting the count after adding the safety read
  • Missing the blitz because eyes lock on key too early
  • Reading motion after the snap instead of before
  • Losing Stage 1 habits when cognitive load increases
Advancement Criteria

All 8 Stage 2 milestones at 80%+ across three sessions. Stage 1 milestones maintained. Motion ID correct on 8 of 10 reps consistently. See Page 7.

"Safeties don't lie. Corners do." — Always look to the safeties first.

Stage 3
Outside Corners + Space — Throwing Receivers Open
Typical: Junior
S · O · C · K · S
SOCKS Focus: S + O + K + S
What Stage 3 Builds: This is where the QB transitions from reacting to anticipating. He reads the outside corner's foot and hip to identify leverage — and understands that leverage tells him WHERE space is about to be. The final S (Space) comes fully online here. Numbers tell you who. Leverage tells you where.
Coaching Cue
"Find the space."
Teaching Goals — Chronological Order
  1. Reading Outside Corner Leverage — Foot and Hip New Skill
    Where is the CB's outside foot? Inside leverage = CB shading inside receiver = outside space is open. Outside leverage = inside is open. Teach with still images first — ask "where is the space?" before "is he open?"
  2. What Leverage Means for Route Outcomes Concept
    Every route attacks a specific space. The slant attacks inside space. The CB's leverage tells you whether the route can win that space BEFORE the receiver runs it. Leverage shows where the window is — and where it isn't.
  3. Reading the Grass, Not the Man Space Concept
    Ask the QB: "Where is the grass?" before "Who is open?" This shifts post-snap vision from person-tracking to space-tracking. The QB becomes coverage-agnostic — he attacks space, not matchups. This is TWTDGY in action.
  4. Combining Apex Trigger + CB Leverage Integration
    The complete Stage 3 read: safeties → count → find Apex → read CB leverage → identify space → throw or give. Apex trigger and CB leverage happen simultaneously — not as a sequence. Drill until the picture is instant.
  5. Throwing Receivers Open — Before Separation Elite Concept
    QB throws to where the receiver is going to be. The slant window opens when Apex triggers and CB has inside leverage. QB throws into that space immediately — ball and route break happen together. Trust the leverage read.
  6. Motion as a Confirmation Tool Motion
    At Stage 3, motion confirms what safeties and count predicted. Man follower = confirmed man — execute with confidence. Zone rotation = confirmed zone — find the grass. Motion is now part of the pre-snap picture, not an afterthought.
  7. Full Throw Clock — 1.8 / 2.2 / 2.6 Seconds Timing
    Introduce the complete three-tier clock. The QB must know which clock applies to each play BEFORE the snap. The clock is pre-loaded, not decided mid-play. "This is a 1.8s play — I throw on the Apex trigger, period."
  8. Field Position and Hash Awareness Situational
    85% of snaps from a hash. The boundary CB has less room — compressed by the sideline. Hash changes leverage reads: a boundary CB with outside leverage has nowhere to recover. Look at the hash before identifying leverage.
Stage 3 Milestones
  • CB leverage read (inside vs. outside) correctly — 8 of 10 film
  • Open grass identified on any pre-snap still in 3 seconds
  • Apex trigger + CB leverage as simultaneous read — 7 of 10 live
  • Ball released into window before separation — 6 of 10 timed reps
  • Motion used correctly as confirmation — 8 of 10 reps with motion
  • Correct throw clock pre-loaded for each play before snap
  • Hash position adjustment applied and explained verbally
  • Stages 1 and 2 milestones maintained at 80%+
Common Mistakes at Stage 3
  • Reading the receiver instead of the defender's leverage
  • Waiting for separation before throwing — window is gone
  • Reading Apex and CB as a sequence instead of simultaneously
  • Ignoring hash position when evaluating leverage
  • Using the wrong throw clock for the play concept
Advancement Criteria

All 8 Stage 3 milestones at 80%+ across three sessions. The anticipatory throw must be demonstrated in live reps, not just drills. Stages 1 and 2 fully maintained. See Page 7.

Stage 4
Full SOCKS — Anticipation, Situational Mastery & Deployment
Typical: Senior
S · O · C · K · S
SOCKS Focus: Full System
What Stage 4 Builds: The complete operating system. The QB is no longer reacting to anything — he is anticipating everything. Pressure indicators, rotation, disguise, and situational context all live here. Motion is deployed strategically. TWTDGY is the operating philosophy on every snap.
Coaching Cue
"Know what's coming."
Teaching Goals — Chronological Order
  1. Pressure Recognition — Reading Intent, Not Position New Skill
    Add pressure indicators to the pre-snap scan: safeties rolling down, LBs creeping, CB press, body language. The QB reads intent, not just alignment. "Is this defense trying to blitz me?" must be answered before the snap.
  2. Coverage Disguise — What Changes Post-Snap Advanced Concept
    Elite defenses show one coverage and rotate to another post-snap. The QB's response is not to ID the final coverage — it is to identify what the rotation creates. A rotating safety opens a window somewhere. Where? That is the question.
  3. Deploying Motion Strategically Motion — Advanced
    Stage 4 teaches the QB to deploy motion with intention. Jet motion to ID man. Orbit motion to see if LB tracks the back. At Stage 4, the QB can suggest or call motion checks at the LOS based on what he sees.
  4. Full Situational Awareness — The Complete Framework Situational Mastery
    All four pillars fully integrated: Down & distance, score & clock, field position, hash & boundary. 3rd & 3 against Cover 1 on the boundary hash means slant is the gift and the clock is 1.8 seconds. The QB knows this before he walks to the line.
  5. Anticipatory Throws — Trusting the Pre-Snap Picture Elite Skill
    QB throws based on the entire pre-snap picture — before the trigger fires, before the route breaks. He trusts the process. This is only possible when Stages 1-3 are completely automatic. It comes from reps, film, and trust.
  6. Elite Timing — Sub-2.8 Second Snap-to-Throw Timing
    At Stage 4, sub-2.8s is the byproduct of automatic pre-snap processing. Track snap-to-throw in practice with a stopwatch. If consistently above 2.8s, the pre-snap process has a gap. Use it as a diagnostic tool.
  7. Film Study as an Operating Habit Leadership
    Stage 4 QBs do not wait for a coach to show them film. They study opponent tendencies independently: fronts, blitz packages, disguise tells. The QB builds a game-week picture so his SOCKS scan is already filtered through what he knows they will likely do.
  8. TWTDGY as Philosophy, Not Phrase Mastery
    Every snap, before the ball is hiked, the QB has answered: "What is this defense giving me right now, in this situation, on this hash, in this down and distance?" The answer to that question IS the throw. Not the play call. Not the progression. The gift.
Stage 4 Milestones
  • 3+ pressure indicators identified accurately — 8 of 10 film
  • Post-snap rotation correctly anticipated from pre-snap — 7 of 10
  • Motion deployed intentionally with stated expected response
  • Full situation (D&D, score, field, hash) shapes every pre-snap call
  • Anticipatory throw before separation — 7 of 10 live reps
  • Snap-to-throw under 2.8s on drop-back reads — 8 of 10 timed
  • Presents one opponent tendency independently before game week
  • All Stages 1-3 milestones maintained — zero regression
Common Mistakes at Stage 4
  • Over-thinking disguise — pre-snap scan still answers the question
  • Deploying motion without clear intent or expected response
  • Situational pressure creating hesitation — trust the process
  • Regression in Stage 1 habits under game pressure
  • Trying to make the perfect throw instead of the right throw
Stage 4 is Not a Destination

Stage 4 is an operating level maintained through consistent film study, rep quality, and self-evaluation. A Stage 4 QB who stops doing Stage 1 habits will slide back. The foundation never stops mattering.

Evaluation System
How to Grade, What to Teach Next, and When to Advance
Mastery-Based Progression
Earned — Not Given
Evaluation Philosophy
Mastery, Not Time

No QB advances based on time in the system or grade. Advancement requires demonstrated mastery — 80%+ across three separate evaluation sessions.

Three Contexts Required

(1) Film room identification, (2) Cadence/walk-through reads, (3) Live reps. Passing the film room but failing live reps = not mastered.

No Regression

Advancing stages does not retire prior milestones. Stage 3 QBs must still demonstrate Stage 1 and 2 milestones at 80%+.

In-Practice Quick Grade — The 3-Question Coach Check
Q1: Did he have a plan?

Before snap: could he verbalize his key? Know down & distance? Was the throw clock pre-loaded?

Yes: Pre-snap process working.
No: Return to Stage 1/2 verbal reps.

Q2: Was the decision correct?

Post-snap: did he throw or give correctly based on the key? Did the ball go to the right place?

Yes: Decision-making sound.
No: Identify whether pre-snap read or post-snap reaction failed — different fixes.

Q3: Was it on time?

Did ball come out in the correct throw clock window? Did he hold the ball after making the right read?

Yes: Timing calibrated.
No: Pre-snap process is too slow. Scan speed is the gap.
